ZAKO Technical Overview §1 — Introduction
§1

Introduction —
The Sovereign Network

ZAKO is a sovereign network OS. A device running ZAKO is not a terminal. It is a full network — a collection of named participants (processes, Islands, daemons) exchanging signed, conserved records over a single protocol at every scale.

1.1 The Design Premise

ZAKO treats harsh conditions — no internet, failing battery, SMS-only connectivity, no cloud — as its design baseline, not its edge case. The system is designed to sustain the most critical exchange under the most challenging conditions. Every architectural decision follows from this premise.

The most important exchanges in people's lives — payments, health records, work records, credentials — happen in the places where infrastructure is least reliable. ZAKO's architecture is the answer to that condition.

1.2 One Protocol at Every Scale

The same protocol that governs two daemons exchanging power signals inside a single device also governs two people exchanging a payment over SMS. This is not a coincidence — it is a structural choice. The fractal architecture table below defines the six scales at which Telux operates:

DistributionMeta-network of sovereign networks · SIMBA admission · ATTEST chains
NewgroupMulti-party shared context · founding CREATE hash · countersigned records
Device ↔ DevicePeer exchange · BitPads frames · offline-capable · any carrier
DeviceSovereign network · 3 Telux daemons · Outstack · Islands
Sub-entityIsland slot · access-controlled · chain-hashed · capability-gated
Recordframe_hash · chain_hash · ed25519 signature · BLAKE3

1.3 Core Invariants

Conservation Invariant

For every batch of exchange records, signed flows sum to zero. This is enforced at the wire level by BitLedger. It cannot be bypassed by application code.

Immutability Invariant

Records written to telux-ledgerd are never modified. Retakes, corrections, and reversals produce new records alongside originals. The chain is the proof.

ZAKO Technical Overview §2 — Telux Protocol
§2

Telux — The Full-Stack
Exchange Protocol

"Tel" — to communicate. Exchange is the essence of communication. Telux is the full-stack system that makes authenticated, conserved, sovereign exchange possible at every scale.

2.1 Why Exchange Is the Foundation

Every meaningful thing a device does involves exchange: a payment is an exchange of value; a work record is an exchange of obligation; a capability grant is an exchange of access; a power mode transition is an exchange of state between Outstack and every listening process.

Exchange requires three things to be trustworthy: identity (who you are exchanging with), record (the exchange must be provable after the fact), and conservation (what is given and what is received must balance). These three requirements define Telux's entire architecture.

2.2 The Telux Daemon Triad

Three CRITICAL-class daemons form the exchange infrastructure. They operate in all power modes, including Emergency. They are never gated by Outstack.

DaemonRoleClassWrite Policy
telux-ledgerdAppend-only ledger · chain-hash computation · BLAKE3 · record validationCRITICALfsync before ACK
telux-identdDID operations · ed25519 signing · capability grants · revocations · SIMBA admissionCRITICALsynchronous
telux-sharedbSelective disclosure · RESTRICT_FORWARD enforcement · peer record exportINTERACTIVEasync · gated in Emergency

2.3 Exchange Session Lifecycle

A bilateral exchange between two parties follows a defined cycle regardless of carrier:

1.  IDENTIFY       Both parties present DIDs · telux-identd verifies
2.  GRANT          Counterparty is granted disclosure scope · GRANT record
3.  SEND / RECEIVE Exchange records are composed and signed
4.  POST           telux-ledgerd writes, fsync, computes chain_hash
5.  ACK            RECEIVE record produced · chain_hash confirmed
6.  SETTLE         Conservation check: flows across accounts sum to zero
7.  SEAL           Compound group closed · final chain_hash anchored

2.4 Newgroup Exchange

A newgroup is a multi-party shared exchange context — a group invoice pool, a shared work record, a collective agreement. Its founding record is a CREATE (task_code=0x01) whose frame_hash becomes the shared truth anchor. All subsequent records in the newgroup reference this anchor. Any member can verify any record in the newgroup by following the chain back to the founding hash, offline, without any external service.

Offline Is Not Disconnection

When a ZAKO device has no external carrier, it is not "offline" — it is operating as a self-contained sovereign network. Internally it is fully functional, fully conserved, fully exchanging. When a carrier becomes available, two sovereign networks exchange. There is no server to reconnect to.

ZAKO Technical Overview §4 — Identity and Sovereignty
§4

Identity and
Sovereignty Model

Identity in ZAKO exists to enable exchange. It is not a feature — it is the prerequisite for trustworthy exchange between parties who may have never met.

4.1 Decentralised Identifiers — did:key

Every ZAKO participant (Sovereign, daemon, SIMBA Node, newgroup node) is identified by a W3C DID using the did:key method. A DID is derived directly from a public key — no external registry, no certificate authority, no network lookup required:

did:key:z6MkBASE58BTC(ed25519_public_key)

# Verification: possession of the public key is sufficient.
# Valid for as long as the keypair is valid.
# Offline-verifiable by any counterparty at any time.

4.2 Key Material and Security

The Sovereign's signing key is generated on-device. In hardware-backed deployments (Keymaster 4.0 / TrustZone), the private key never leaves the Trusted Execution Environment. In software deployments, the key is encrypted at rest and zeroed in memory on device lock.

Key rotation is accomplished through a chain of BIND (task_code=0x0D) and ATTEST (task_code=0x0E) records: the old key signs the new key's DID; the new key attests continuity. The chain is locally verifiable without any external authority.

4.3 Capability Grants

Access to any Island or sub-entity is governed by explicit capability grants. A grant is a signed GRANT (task_code=0x08) record in the Identity sub-entity, specifying the grantee's DID, the granted scopes, and any time bounds:

Grant ScopeEffectIssued By
read_islandRead access to a named Island's recordsSovereign
write_islandWrite access to a named Island's recordsSovereign
write_exchangePost records to Exchange sub-entitySovereign
share_recordSelective disclosure of specific recordsSovereign
simba_nodeSIMBA Node participation rightstelux-identd (post-admission)

Grants are revocable at any time. A REVOKE (task_code=0x09) record is posted to the Identity sub-entity; telux-identd enforces the revocation immediately. No grace period. No retry window for the revoked node.

4.4 Credential Issuance and Verification

Credentials are ATTEST records in the Identity sub-entity. An authority-issued credential carries the issuing authority's DID as source_did. A self-certified competency carries the Sovereign's own DID. Any counterparty can verify a credential chain offline:

  1. Verify the ATTEST record signature against source_did
  2. Verify the referenced mastery record against the Sovereign's DID
  3. Optionally verify the assessment record referenced by the mastery record

No network call required at any step. The chain from assessment → mastery → credential is entirely locally verifiable.

ZAKO Technical Overview §5 — The Ledger
§5

The Ledger —
telux-ledgerd

telux-ledgerd is the append-only ledger daemon. It is CRITICAL class — it runs in all five power modes, including Emergency. It is the authoritative record of everything that has happened on a ZAKO device.

5.1 Write Guarantees

Every write to telux-ledgerd follows a strict sequence before the ACK is issued to the caller:

1.  Validate frame structure and mandatory fields
2.  Verify ed25519 signature against source_did
3.  Check task_code against caller's declared record model (SIMBA Nodes)
4.  Compute frame_hash = BLAKE3(frame_bytes)
5.  Compute chain_hash = BLAKE3(frame_hash ‖ prev_chain_hash)
6.  Write record to Island partition
7.  fsync — mandatory before ACK
8.  Issue ACK with frame_hash and chain_hash to caller

Step 7 (fsync) is fixed. It cannot be changed by distribution configuration or Sovereign preference. A write is not confirmed until it has been synced to persistent storage.

5.2 Chain Integrity

Each Island partition maintains its own chain. The chain_hash of each record commits to all prior records in that partition:

frame_hash[n]  = BLAKE3(frame_bytes[n])
chain_hash[n]  = BLAKE3(
  frame_hash[n] ‖ chain_hash[n-1]
)

Tampering with any record invalidates all subsequent chain_hashes. The chain serves as a tamper-evident seal without requiring any external validator.

No Mutable Balances

ZAKO never stores computed aggregates (total XP, running balance, cumulative score) as mutable fields. All totals are computed at read time from the full record set. The ledger is the balance.

5.3 Island Partitioning

5.4 Compound Records

Multi-leg transactions (e.g., payment + content access grant, assessment score + mastery determination) are expressed as compound groups. The first record has Completeness=1. Continuation records carry group_sep=1111 headers. The final record has Completeness=0, closing the group. telux-ledgerd guarantees compound groups are written atomically — a group is either fully committed or fully absent.

5.5 JOURNAL Invariant

Records written to the Journal sub-entity (sub_entity=3, file_sep=3) must carry restrict_fwd=1. This is enforced by telux-ledgerd: a Journal record submitted without restrict_fwd=1 is rejected. The Journal partition is additionally sealed by SELinux policy — no SIMBA Node capability grant can override this seal.

ZAKO Technical Overview §6 — Outstack Architecture
§6

Outstack —
Power Architecture

Outstack was born from Telux's operating constraint: exchange cannot survive if the device cannot survive. Outstack treats the power budget as a ledger and process class as law.

6.1 The Five Power Modes

ModeCodeBattery ThresholdProcess Classes Permitted
Full Power0x0> STANDARD_FLOOR (50%)ALL — no restrictions
Standard0x1> CONSERVE_FLOOR (20%)CRITICAL · INTERACTIVE · BACKGROUND · DEFERRED
Conservation0x2> LOW_FLOOR (10%)CRITICAL · INTERACTIVE · BACKGROUND (deferred)
Critical Reserve0x3> SHUTDOWN_FLOOR (3%)CRITICAL · INTERACTIVE
Emergency0x4≤ SHUTDOWN_FLOOR or thermalCRITICAL only

6.2 Mode Transition Sequence

Mode transitions are not instantaneous. outstack-powerd follows a defined sequence on every transition:

1.  Threshold crossing detected (battery %, thermal, charging state)
2.  Pre-transition confirmation sample (prevents flip-flop at boundaries)
3.  MODE_ENTER C0 signal (slot 0x03) emitted to system bus
4.  POWER_CHANGE record written to System sub-entity (sub_entity=0)
5.  Gate records written atomically with the POWER_CHANGE record
6.  outstack-powerd internal mode state updated
7.  All ZAKO daemons receive MODE_ENTER and update execution policy

Steps 3–5 are atomic. A daemon receiving MODE_ENTER can be certain the ledger record was committed before the signal arrived.

6.3 Hysteresis

To prevent mode oscillation at boundaries, Outstack applies a HYSTERESIS_BAND (default: 3%) to all threshold crossings:

If CONSERVE_FLOOR = 20%: Conservation begins at 20%. Standard resumes at 23%. A device sitting at 21% does not oscillate between Standard and Conservation.

6.4 Power Sources Tracked

DimensionMeasurementSampling Role
BatteryPercentage (0–100), voltage (mV)Primary budget signal · mode gate trigger
ThermalDevice temp (°C), CPU thermal zoneRate-of-draw signal · Emergency trigger
CPU drawInstantaneous wattage by classDraw accounting · class priority adjustment
ChargingRate (mW), source (USB/wireless/solar)Budget replenishment · upward transition signal

6.5 The Gate Mechanism

When a mode transition would gate a process class, outstack-powerd submits a REVOKE record through telux-identd targeting the execution capability of all processes in the gated class. A companion GATE record is written to the System sub-entity with the gated class bitmask. The gate is a ledger event — it is provable, timestamped, and signed.

7.3 Class Declaration and Registration

Every process declares its class in an ASSIGN record written to the System sub-entity when registered with outstack-powerd. The record carries value = process_class_code. A process that executes without a valid registration is treated as BACKGROUND. A process that attempts CRITICAL actions without a CRITICAL registration has those actions queued at INTERACTIVE priority.

When a gate is applied, outstack-powerd does not use arbitrary interruption. It signals affected processes via the MODE_ENTER C0 signal and expects them to reach their declared suspension point within the standard window:

ZAKO Technical Overview §9 — Exchange Engine and Peers
§9

Exchange Engine,
Newgroups, and Peers

9.1 The Exchange Engine

The Exchange Engine is the CRITICAL-class component that manages bilateral and multilateral financial and record exchange. It is the primary interface between the Sovereign's financial life and the ledger. It enforces the conservation invariant at the application layer (above the wire-level enforcement in telux-ledgerd).

The Exchange Engine's core settlement loop runs at CRITICAL class. Its UI presentation layer (invoicing, payment approval dialogs) runs at INTERACTIVE. Its background sync (pending invoice reconciliation, receipt matching) runs at BACKGROUND.

9.2 Bilateral Exchange — SEND / RECEIVE Cycle

A bilateral exchange between Sovereign A and Sovereign B produces a symmetric pair of records. Each party holds their own copy in their own ledger, referenced by the same frame_hash as a shared truth anchor:

# Sovereign A ledger
task_code    = 0x1C  PAY
account_pair = 1001  (Asset → Liability)
value        = 2400  (MK 240.00)
dest_did     = Sovereign_B_DID
frame_hash   = a3f9…c012
# Sovereign B ledger
task_code    = 0x1E  RECEIVE
account_pair = 0110  (Receivable → Income)
value        = 2400
source_did   = Sovereign_A_DID
ref_hash     = a3f9…c012

The frame_hash serves as the shared truth anchor. Either party can present their record; the counterparty can verify it references the same exchange without network access.

9.3 Newgroup Model

A newgroup is a multi-party shared exchange context. It is founded by a single CREATE record (task_code=0x01). The frame_hash of the founding record becomes the newgroup's truth anchor — a stable identifier that all subsequent records in the newgroup reference.

Eventtask_codeDescription
Found0x01 CREATEEstablishes the newgroup; frame_hash becomes the group anchor
Join0x06 BINDMember joins; BIND record references the group anchor
Postany verbMember posts a record; must carry the group anchor as ref_hash
Countersign0x0E ATTESTNewgroup node countersigns a member's record for collective authority
Resolve0x15 COMMITCloses a newgroup context (agreement fulfilled, invoice settled)

9.4 Period Tallies

At the close of a defined period (week, month, custom interval), the Exchange Engine produces a Tally — a compound record group summarising all exchange activity in that period. Tally records use group_sep=63 (the reserved Period Close value). A Tally must be the last record written in its partition before the period boundary. It does not replace the underlying records — it is a summary that references them.

Hosted Newgroup Infrastructure

A Ledger Node (defined in the Infrastructure Extension Profile) can host a newgroup on behalf of its members. It countersigns member records within 2 seconds of receipt. The Ledger Node's countersignature is an ATTEST record — itself a ledger entry. The Sovereign maintains a full local copy of all newgroup records they participated in.

ZAKO Technical Overview §11 — SIMBA Integration Standard
§11

SIMBA — Service
Integration Standard

Service Integration for Mobile Business Applications. SIMBA defines the compliance requirements an external service must satisfy to be admitted as a participating node on the ZAKO device network.

11.1 The SIMBA Node Model

An admitted service becomes a SIMBA Node — a named, registered participant with its own DID, a declared process class, a capability grant from the Sovereign, and a signed manifest on file with the distribution. A SIMBA Node is not a guest. It is subject to exactly the same protocol rules as native ZAKO daemons.

ZAKO Technical Overview §12 — Network and Carrier Layer
§12

Network Layer — Carriers,
Offline Model, Infrastructure

12.1 Carriers as Roads

ZAKO does not live inside a carrier network. SMS, Bluetooth LE, QR code, and IP are carriers — transport surfaces that BitPads frames may travel over. The carrier does not confer validity. The ed25519 signature does. A payment sent over SMS is as valid as one sent over IP; a credential shared by QR code is as verifiable as one delivered over a TLS API.

CarrierFrame EncodingOffline CapableTypical Use
IP / Mobile DataBitPads binary (preferred)No (requires data)Full sync · newgroup posts · content delivery
SMSBitPads compact (CRC-15 + base encoding)2G sufficientPayment exchange · record delivery · emergency comms
Bluetooth LEBitPads binaryYes — device-to-devicePeer exchange · point-of-sale · credential presentation
QR Codepads-v1 URL (#1pa/ scheme)Yes — scan-and-displayCredential presentation · invoice initiation · grant transfer

12.2 Offline-First Guarantees

ZAKO implementations must satisfy these offline-first requirements:

Records are written, signed, and chain-hashed on-device before any transmission occurs.
Credentials are verifiable from the Sovereign's public key alone — no resolver.
Academy content is cached offline before study begins — no mid-lesson network call.
Exchange sessions may complete entirely offline; sync occurs when connectivity is available.
Assessment records and completion events are posted to local ledger before any upload.
No ZAKO core service may require network connectivity for its primary function.

12.3 Infrastructure Extension Profile — Node Roles

ZAKO distributions may deploy infrastructure nodes that extend the network without violating sovereign principles. Four roles are defined:

Content Addressing

Content delivered by a Content Node is identified by BLAKE3(content_bytes). A Sovereign's device verifies the content against the stored hash before presenting it. Corrupt or substituted content is rejected before rendering.
